Extropy and why I like it

A mental heuristic I find useful is the concept of entropy. At a reductionist level, the world is chaotic—systems left to themselves cannot decrease entropy. Only humans, through intelligence and rationality, act as an organizing force to push back.

Things tend to rot, decay, and decline. It’s humbling: the universe is indifferent, but we’re not. We have a duty to each other to make our corner of the world a little less chaotic.

Recently on X I saw extropianism defined as the continuous drive for human improvement—reducing entropy wherever we can. In a sense, whiggism for the 21st century: celebrating progress, adventure, and the belief that we can push forward.

Elon Musk likens humanity to a flickering candle in the wind. To him, that’s why sustainable energy, free speech, and interplanetary travel matter. Critics may scoff, but if we crack effective spaceflight, that’s a win for extropy.

One inspiring idea is self-healing materials. Imagine potholes that fix themselves—no more road tax hikes, no more cracked rims, just smooth roads. Self-repairing phones, furniture, paint that mends its scratches… a world aligned with our anti-entropic drive.

The possibilities are near-limitless. A laptop that lasts decades or forever, homes that maintain themselves, infrastructure that never fails. Extropy offers the philosophical wellspring for engineering a world that fights back against decay—and powers our next great leaps.
