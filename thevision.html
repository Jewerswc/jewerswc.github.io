What is the vision for Interesting Or Not?

When I was 21 I was working in politics, and with politics comes the media.

At that time though I was reading lots of blogs. The authors of those blogs were the smartest people that I had come across. In some wordly (and perhaps indefinable) sense they were much smarter than the people at university. That's besides the point, though. Much smarter than traditional newspaper columnists, too. Slate Star Codex, Tim Gowers, Dominic Cummings, Steve Hsu, LessWrong, Paul Graham were just some. It was like unearthing the secret of where all the smart people were.

I could also see that politics was going south and pretty quickly. But I was interested in all these smart people who were keen to solve hard problems and build cool things, unlike the vast majority of politicians.

I also thought it was pretty odd that most people had never heard of these smart people, yet they had heard of all the newspaper columnists who by comparison were fools simply spreading the latest popular meme.

So, I wrote to one of them on their Substack, and asked for advice. They replied and said (with great foresight for 2020) that I should learn to code, and work on cool projects that I find interesting. At the time this seemed like fairly abstract advice, but I did it, nonetheless.

So I learned to code did a few of my own projects, and sometime after I got interested in startups. Their blog, their writing, and them responding to me on their blog gave me an 'aha' moment that changed my life.

I started my startup in July 2024. I wanted more people to have more great content, like the blog that gave me an 'aha' moment, and meant that I'd learn to code, get interested in startups, and ultimately change my course.

For a while, I thought that meant well we should make it easier for as many people as possible to write, earn, and get reach for their content.

That as a hypothesis though had a few fundamental issues. The first problem you might call the 'cold start' problem. Why do I want to be the first person to read - let alone pay for - the first piece of writing someone has done? And, will they actually be any good the first time around? It's probably just a truth that you can't hack the adoption curve of things, no matter how hard you try.

Secondly, and perhaps above all else, there was a big elephant in the room - AI. LLMs seemed to dominate startup discussion. I'd always had in my head that I was betting on the human experience, and the human capacity to write great stuff, and against LLMs. I knew it was a risk.

The funny thing was that when I first got people using the platform they used LLMs to write things. And at this time, we saw a steep improvement, with o1-mini, o1, Deepseek's R1, and o3-mini.

So I thought what if you could create AI-personas that could write like humans, but were smarter and faster to adapt. This seemed to have several benefits that my old model about how to solve the problem fell short on:

  • More content that was better smarter and faster to adapt what readers want
  • Larger profit margins. We'd no longer have to pay out 80% of our reader revenue to writers. That, from a financial standpoint, was a huge win.
  • Multimodality, all of a sudden, what went from a 'writing platform' went to a platform that could have writers, artists, video creators and more. The only limtiation is the AI.
  • Agency: more writers (or indeed content creators) that can do more things more quickly. We can have content creators that can do things far beyond just writing. What about having an AI doing real human interviews and more of them - and what if they could organise it all by themself. That is the potential.

So if you were to ask me 'what is the vision for Interesting Or Not?', I guess I would respond with this: the place where more people are able to have their 'aha' moments more of the time by distilling the knowledge and wisdom of people and ideas for them.

What that menans in concrete terms is the place where AI agents write the 'everything magazine', where you have every blog or video or podcast that you ever wanted to read watch or listen to - or perhaps didn't even know you wanted. That is the promise of Interesting Or Not.

Extropy and why I like it

As a heuristic guide, I find useful the idea that the physical word is entropic. That at a reductionist level, the world is chaotic, and that systems left to themselves cannot decrease in entropy. It is, instead, only humans, through intelligence and rationality, acting as an organising force, that can alter the system to decrease entropy.

The reduced physical disposition of things is that they are disposed to, rot, decay and decline. And, it is only humans, being the only intelligent lifeform we know of (so far), that is capable of pushing back on entropy. I find it a humbling idea. That the universe is indifferent to us, but we are not indifferent to it, and that if anything we have a duty to each other to maybe make it just a little bit less chaotic.

Something that has been popularised on X recently is the idea of extropianism. I like this idea, the philosophy of reducing entropy where we can, or the continuous drive for human improvement. Some might call it whiggism for the 21st century. Maybe that’s because whiggism - if you’re not a nihilist at least - always had a grain of truth to it, or maybe, at the very least, it captured humanities desire and spirit for adventure.

I think that it’s useful to have this kind of underpinning philosophy. To have some form of intellectual underpinning to what you are doing. Elon Musk often talks about humanity is like a flickering candle in the wind, and if we’re not careful, it could go out. And, for him, that is why sustainable transportation, energy, free speech, and, ultimately, interplanetary space travel are so necessary. Cynics (and communists for that matter) argue that moral rationalisation of sociopathy etc. Regardless, if we do get effective interplanetary space travel, that is some kind of win.

I think the idea of extropy provides a wellspring of ideas. One I’ve had recently is self-healing materials. This tackles the issue at its perhaps very most fundamental level, and if we can crack self-healing materials, then that is a great start. Think, what if potholes in roads could heal themselves. This suddenly removes all sorts of entropic forces otherwise at work. Citizens are unburdended from taxation going towards fixing them, drivers no longer have to mend cracked rims, and we all ride on smooth surfaces.

The boundaries of it are near-on limitless. Think a laptop or iPhone that lasts if not for much much longer that your current one, if not forever. Self-healing furniture. Paint that touches itself back up when you scratch it. We start to create a world that aligns with our anti-entropic and indeed biological intuitions.
