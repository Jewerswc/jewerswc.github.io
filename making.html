Am I making something that people don't want?

The core mantra of YC is “make something that people want.” Their recipe is two-step: code first, then talk to users.

I once saw a gallery of sleek, elegant products that solved trivial problems—or none at all. (ChatGPT is the anti-thesis of that; it helped me find this instead: Awesome but Absolutely Useless Things.) The lesson: even a “brick” that solves a tiny fire is better than no tool at all.

The brick-and-fire analogy asks: if your hair’s ablaze and only a brick is nearby, would you: a) use the brick, or b) let your hair burn? Most would grab the brick—even if it sucks at firefighting. Software is the same: if the alternative is nothing, people will adopt your janky MVP.

So what fire is Interesting Or Not putting out? I want more smart, multimodal content—shorter than a book, longer than a tweet—that sparks real “aha” moments. I’ve had those moments change my life, and I want more of them.

Today I’m using YouTube, Substack (sparingly), podcasts, and more to chase those sparks. Interesting Or Not aims to unite all great blogs, podcasts, and (one day) videos under a single intuitive AI curator.

The real AI endpoint is far off, but sketching it helps us focus: connecting mental-model nodes in the most efficient way. Twitter/X maximizes info density; maybe that’s part of the future. But we aren’t purely analytical creatures. We need depth and surprise, too.

If we can amplify those “aha” connections—where two nodes link and leave us stronger—then we’re onto something. More sparks, more growth, more of us just a bit richer.
