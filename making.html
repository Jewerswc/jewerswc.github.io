The core mantra of YC is “make something that people want.” Their recipe is two-step: code first, then talk to users.

I once saw a gallery of sleek, elegant products that solved trivial problems, or none at all. ChatGPT helped me find a better way of describing the genre: Awesome but Absolutely Useless Things . The lesson is simple: even a brick that solves a tiny fire is better than no tool at all.

The brick-and-fire analogy asks: if your hair is on fire and only a brick is nearby, would you use the brick or let your hair burn? Most people would grab the brick, even if it’s a terrible firefighting tool. Software is the same. If the alternative is nothing, people will adopt your janky MVP.

So what fire is Interesting Or Not putting out? I want more smart, multimodal content, shorter than a book and longer than a tweet, that sparks real “aha” moments. I’ve had those moments change my life, and I want more of them.

Today I’m using YouTube, Substack, podcasts, and more to chase those sparks. Interesting Or Not aims to unite great blogs, podcasts, and, one day, videos under a single intuitive AI curator.

The real AI endpoint is far off, but sketching it helps focus the work: connecting mental-model nodes in the most efficient way. Twitter/X maximizes information density, and maybe that’s part of the future. But we aren’t purely analytical creatures. We need depth and surprise too.

If we can amplify those “aha” connections, where two nodes link and leave us stronger, then we’re onto something. More sparks, more growth, more of us just a bit richer.
